What Is an IP Address?

An IP (Internet Protocol) address is a unique number assigned to every device connected to the internet. Think of it like a postal address — without it, data packets wouldn't know where to go. Every time you load a website, your browser sends requests from your IP address, and the server sends responses back to it.

There are two versions in use today: IPv4 and IPv6.

The world ran out of IPv4 addresses around 2011, which is why IPv6 was introduced. Most modern networks support both simultaneously — a setup called dual stack.

What Does an IP Address Reveal?

When someone has your IP address, they can typically find out:

  • Country — very accurate (99%+)
  • Region / State — usually accurate
  • City — approximate (often the nearest exchange point)
  • ISP / Carrier — who provides your internet
  • Timezone — inferred from location

What they cannot find from your IP alone: your exact street address, your name, or any personal information. That data requires a court order to your ISP.

💡 Tip: The city shown in an IP lookup is often the location of your ISP's network exchange, not where you physically are. This is especially common with mobile carriers and business connections.

Public vs Private IP Addresses

There are two types of IP addresses you'll encounter:

Public IP

Your public IP is what the rest of the internet sees. It's assigned by your ISP and is the address returned by any "what is my IP" tool. All devices on your home network share the same public IP through a process called NAT (Network Address Translation).

Private IP

Private IPs are used inside local networks (your home WiFi, office network). They're not routable on the public internet. The reserved ranges are:

RangeCommon Use
10.0.0.0 – 10.255.255.255Large corporate networks
172.16.0.0 – 172.31.255.255Mid-size networks
192.168.0.0 – 192.168.255.255Home routers (most common)

When you check your IP on a laptop connected to home WiFi, you'll get the public IP — not the 192.168.x.x private address your router assigned to your laptop.

How to Find Your IP Address

Using a tool (easiest)

Visit sysutil.dev/ip — your IP is displayed instantly, along with country, region, city and timezone.

On Linux / Mac (terminal)

# External IP (what the internet sees)
curl ifconfig.me

# Or
curl -s https://api.ipify.org

# Local network IP
ip addr show        # Linux
ifconfig            # Mac/Linux

On Windows (Command Prompt)

# External IP
curl ifconfig.me

# Local IP
ipconfig

How to Look Up Any IP Address

To look up geolocation and details for any IP address:

  1. Go to sysutil.dev/ip
  2. Enter the IP address in the input field
  3. Click Look Up
  4. See country, region, city, coordinates and timezone

You can also look up IP addresses from the command line:

# Using curl with a free geo API
curl -s https://ipapi.co/8.8.8.8/json/ | python3 -m json.tool

# Or using dig to find the hostname
dig -x 8.8.8.8 +short

How to Hide Your IP Address

There are several ways to mask your public IP:

VPN (Recommended)

A VPN routes all your traffic through a server in another location. Websites see the VPN server's IP instead of yours. Good for privacy, streaming geo-restricted content, and using public WiFi safely.

Tor Browser

Routes traffic through multiple volunteer-operated relays, making it very difficult to trace back to you. Much slower than a VPN but more anonymous. Used by journalists and privacy advocates.

Proxy Server

Similar to a VPN but typically only for web traffic and less secure. Free proxies in particular should be avoided as they may log your traffic.

⚠️ Note: Hiding your IP doesn't make you anonymous — websites can still track you through cookies, browser fingerprinting, and logged-in accounts.
